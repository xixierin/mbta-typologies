Methodology

This analysis links land use configuration at MBTA station areas to vehicle miles traveled per capita, using cross-sectional OLS regression, Ward hierarchical clustering, and longitudinal long-difference regression. The goal is to identify whether variation in station-area typology — not density alone — predicts VMT, and whether that variation has policy implications for differentiated implementation of the MBTA Communities Act.

Unit of analysis

Each observation is a 0.5-mile station area buffer around an MBTA Communities station (n=169, excluding Boston). VMT per capita is derived from Massachusetts Vehicle Census odometer readings, aggregated to buffers via block group clipping and area-weighted normalization. The dependent variable is log-transformed to address right skew.

Analytical pipeline

01

Cross-sectional OLS regression

Establishes baseline relationships between land use variables and VMT per capita across station areas. Controls include median household income, young adult share (18–34), and senior share (65+). Housing density is retained as a policy-anchor variable regardless of VIF.

02

Hierarchical clustering (k=6)

Ward.D2 linkage on Euclidean distances across four input variables: activity intensity (housing + job density composite), job mix, land use entropy, and peak transit frequency. VMT is deliberately excluded from clustering inputs to avoid circularity with its role as the regression outcome. Six clusters selected via dendrogram inspection and silhouette analysis.

03

Within-cluster regression

OLS models estimated separately for each cluster typology, allowing the relationship between land use variables and VMT to vary by station type. This tests whether density, mix, and entropy operate differently across the typological spectrum.

04

Longitudinal long-difference regression

Changes in VMT between two periods are regressed on changes in land use variables, identifying whether growth in housing or commercial density at station areas corresponds to measurable VMT reductions over time.

Limitations: Spatial autocorrelation has not been tested via Moran's I. Parking supply data may not reflect current conditions. The silhouette score for k=6 reflects cluster overlap visible in the PCA projection (PC1+PC2+PC3 explain 92.8% of clustering variable variance).

Data sources

Massachusetts Vehicle Census — odometer readings aggregated to station area buffers as the primary VMT measure.

MassBuild — housing units and commercial square footage by year, used to construct longitudinal land use change variables.

GTFS / MBTA schedule data — peak hour train frequency as the transit input to clustering.

US Census / ACS — demographic controls (income, age structure).

MassGIS parcels + OSM — land use entropy and street network variables.
